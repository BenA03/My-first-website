My Favorite Chess Openings

What are chess openings?

Chess is a huge game, containing nearly limitless games and move orders. Mathmeticians calculate that just within the first 5 moves from each player, there are 69,352,859,712,417 possible games that could have occured.

Of course, not all of these games place white and black on equal footing. Instead, chess players often play certain lines of moves at the beggining of a game, called an opening, that give their side a chance to best attack, defend, or outmaneuver their opponent.

Each opening has different styles, short-term, and long-term strategies. In this website, I'll be explaining some of my favorite openings and some of the strategic ideas behind each one.

The Nimzo-Larsen Attack

When I am playing with the white pieces, one of my favorite openings is The Nimzo-Larsen Attack. It begins with the move pawn to b3. The idea of this opening is to develop the bishop on the long diagonal (which is often refered to as a Fianchetto), placing pressure on black's e pawn if it is moved to the center, and, over time, targeting the black king if it castles kingside.

According to lichess, a popular online chess site, I win about 49.2% of my Nimzo-Larsen games, draw 7.9% of the time, and lose 42.9% of the time.

The London System

Another opening I play very often with white is called the London System. It begins with the move pawn to d4 and bishop to f4. It is relatively easy opening and good for beginners to learn. The plans of the opening is to push your pawns to c3 and e3, play your other bishop to d3, and your knights to d2 and f3. You can use your strong bishops to create an attack on the enemy king when the time is right. If you are looking to get into chess for the first time, this might be a good opening for you!

According to lichess, a popular online chess site, I win about 54.5% of my London System games, draw 3.9% of the time, and lose 41.6% of the time.

The Caro-Kann

The Caro-Kann is an opening played by black that can be used after white has started the game by playing pawn to e4. The Caro-Kann begins with the move pawn to c6 and then pawn to d5, which tries to attack white's pawn on e4. White can choose to protect the pawn, push it forwards, or take our pawn. All three of these options lead to different and exciting kinds of games!

According to lichess, a popular online chess site, I win about 71.4% of my Caro-Kann, never draw, and lose 28.6% of the time.
